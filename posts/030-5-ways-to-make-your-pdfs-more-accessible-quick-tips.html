5 Ways to Make Your PDFs More Accessible - Quick Tips

PDFs are everywhere, but not everyone can read them easily. People with visual impairments, learning disabilities, or physical limitations often struggle with poorly designed PDFs.

Making your PDFs accessible isn't just the right thing to do—it's also good for business. More people can read your content, and your documents reach a wider audience.

Here are 5 simple ways to make your PDFs more accessible.

Tip 1: Use Clear, Readable Fonts

The Problem: Fancy fonts look nice but are hard to read. Script fonts, decorative fonts, and extremely thin fonts create barriers for many readers.

How to Fix It:

  • Use standard fonts like Arial, Helvetica, or Times New Roman
  • Use font size of at least 12pt for body text
  • Avoid using ALL CAPS for long passages
  • Use bold for emphasis, not italics

Tip 2: Add Alt Text to Images

The Problem: Screen readers can't "see" images. If you don't add alt text, visually impaired users miss important information.

How to Fix It:

  • Describe the image's content and purpose
  • Keep descriptions concise but informative
  • Include context when relevant
  • Mark decorative images as "presentational"

Good Alt Text Examples:

Image Type Bad Alt Text Good Alt Text
Chart "Chart" "Bar chart showing 30% sales increase in Q3"
Logo "Logo" "PDFHub.AI company logo"
Photo "Photo" "Team meeting with 6 people around a table"
Diagram "Diagram" "Flow chart showing PDF conversion process"

Tip 3: Structure with Headings

The Problem: Documents without structure are hard to navigate. Screen reader users can't jump between sections.

How to Fix It:

  • Use H1 for main title
  • Use H2 for major sections
  • Use H3 for sub-sections
  • Use H4 for sub-sub-sections

Why Headings Matter:

Benefit Explanation
Navigation Users can jump between sections
Understanding Clear hierarchy shows document structure
Scanning All users can find information faster
Screen readers Reads headings aloud for navigation

Tip 4: Add Descriptive Hyperlinks

The Problem: "Click here" and "Learn more" don't tell users where the link goes. Screen reader users often navigate links without context.

How to Fix It:

  • Use descriptive link text
  • Tell users where the link goes
  • Avoid "click here" or "read more"
  • Make it clear if the link opens a new window

Good Link Text Examples:

Bad Link Good Link
Click here View our PDF compression guide
Read more Download the full report (PDF, 2MB)
Learn more Visit PDFHub.AI tools page
See details Check our pricing plans

Tip 5: Ensure Sufficient Color Contrast

The Problem: Low contrast between text and background makes documents hard to read. Users with visual impairments or color blindness struggle.

How to Fix It:

  • Use dark text on light backgrounds
  • Avoid light gray text on white backgrounds
  • Avoid red text on green backgrounds
  • Use tools to check contrast ratio

Color Contrast Guidelines:

Contrast WCAG Rating Description
4.5:1 or higher ✅ AA Minimum for accessibility
7:1 or higher ✅ AAA Enhanced accessibility
3:1 or higher ✅ Large text only For large or bold text
Below 3:1 ❌ Fail Not accessible

Frequently Asked Questions

Is making PDFs accessible difficult? No. Most accessibility improvements are simple and take only a few minutes.

Do accessible PDFs look different? No. Accessibility changes are mostly behind the scenes. The document looks the same.

Are accessible PDFs required by law? In many countries, yes. Government agencies, public companies, and educational institutions often require accessibility.

Can I check if my PDF is accessible? Yes. Use the Accessibility Checker in Acrobat Pro or try free online tools.

Does accessibility cost extra? No. Most accessibility improvements are free and built into standard tools.
