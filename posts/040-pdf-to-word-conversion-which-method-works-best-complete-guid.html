PDF to Word Conversion: Which Method Works Best? — Complete Guide (2026)

You need to edit a PDF. Your first instinct is to open it in Word, but the formatting breaks. Or you try an online tool, but you're worried about privacy. How do you choose the right method?

This guide compares five PDF to Word conversion methods in depth, covering their strengths, weaknesses, and ideal use cases. You'll also learn how to handle scanned PDFs and protect sensitive documents.

Why PDF to Word Conversion Remains a Challenge

PDFs are designed to look the same on every device. Word documents, by contrast, use flowable text that adapts when you edit. When a converter translates between these two formats, it must guess where paragraphs begin and end, which font styles map to Word styles, and how to rebuild tables.

The result: Fonts change, tables break, paragraphs split into short lines, and images float away.

But with the right approach, near-perfect conversion is possible. Here are the five main methods, compared.

Method 1: Microsoft Word (Built-in)

If you have Word 2013 or later, you can open PDFs directly — no extra tools required.[citation:1]

How it works:

  1. Open Word, click File > Open
  2. Browse and select your PDF file
  3. Click OK when Word prompts you to convert
  4. Edit the document, then Save As .docx

Best for: Text-based PDFs with simple layouts[citation:1]

Limitations: Complex layouts, multi-column documents, and scanned PDFs may not convert cleanly[citation:4]. Opening a PDF in Word can cause fonts to change, tables to break, and paragraphs to split into short lines[citation:1][citation:4].

Verdict: A quick, free option for simple documents, but not recommended for complex formatting.

Method 2: Online Conversion Tools

Online tools like Smallpdf, iLovePDF, and PDF24 require no installation and are available on any device with a browser. Some, like PDF24, also offer a desktop version that supports fully offline conversion.[citation:4]

How it works:

  1. Visit the tool's website
  2. Upload your PDF
  3. Wait for server-side processing (seconds to a minute)
  4. Download your Word document

Pros:

  • No installation, works anywhere[citation:4]
  • Intuitive interface
  • Some support batch processing

Cons:

  • Privacy risk: Your files are uploaded to third-party servers[citation:4][citation:12]
  • Free versions have file size limits (often 5-25MB) and daily caps[citation:12]
  • Conversion quality varies widely by platform[citation:4]
  • Some add watermarks to converted documents[citation:12]

When to use: Non-sensitive documents where convenience outweighs privacy concerns. For sensitive files, choose a tool that processes locally, such as PDFHub.AI.

Method 3: Professional Desktop Software

Desktop software such as Adobe Acrobat and Foxit PDF Editor convert files locally, without uploading data to the cloud. This makes them safer for commercial and confidential documents.[citation:4]

How it works (Adobe Acrobat):

  1. Open the PDF in Acrobat
  2. Click Export PDF in the right panel
  3. Select Microsoft Word as the export format
  4. Click Export and save the file[citation:5]

Pros:

  • File stays on your device (privacy protection)[citation:4]
  • High-quality format preservation[citation:4]
  • Built-in OCR for scanned documents[citation:4]
  • No file size limits

Cons:

  • Expensive (Acrobat Pro requires a subscription)
  • Installation required
  • Learning curve

Pro tip: To maintain original fonts when converting, ensure fonts are embedded in the PDF before conversion[citation:6].

When to use: Frequent conversions, complex layouts, or sensitive/confidential documents.

Method 4: WPS Office (Local Conversion)

WPS Office integrates PDF to Word conversion directly into its office suite. The core format conversion and OCR processing happen locally on your device, which means your files don't touch third-party servers.[citation:4][citation:12]

How it works:

  1. Open the PDF in WPS Office
  2. Click the Convert menu
  3. Select PDF to Word
  4. The conversion runs locally
  5. Save the resulting document

Pros:

  • Local processing, no cloud upload — essential for sensitive documents[citation:4][citation:12]
  • Built-in OCR for scanned PDFs[citation:4]
  • Good formatting preservation of paragraphs, spacing, font hierarchy, and tables[citation:4][citation:12]

Cons:

  • Free version has daily usage limits
  • Requires installation

When to use: Business documents, contracts, financial records, and other sensitive files.

Method 5: Copy and Paste (Emergency Use Only)

If your PDF is text-based and formatting doesn't matter, you can select text in the PDF viewer, copy it, and paste it into Word.

How it works:

  1. Open the PDF in any PDF viewer
  2. Select all text (Ctrl+A)
  3. Copy (Ctrl+C)
  4. Paste into a new Word document (Ctrl+V)

Pros:

  • Zero software, zero cost
  • Works immediately

Cons:

  • Formatting is completely lost
  • Images and tables don't transfer correctly
  • Only works with text-based PDFs (not scanned)
  • Manual cleanup is required[citation:4]

When to use: Only in emergencies when you need raw text and nothing else.

Special Case: Converting Scanned PDFs (OCR Required)

The Problem: Scanned PDFs are images, not text. Opening a scanned PDF in Word often results in a blank page or uneditable content. Default Windows and macOS PDF viewers do not support OCR.[citation:9]

The Solution: OCR (Optical Character Recognition) detects text in images and converts it to editable content.

Option A: EaseUS PDF Editor (Windows)

This software integrates OCR technology to convert scanned PDFs to editable Word documents on your local machine.[citation:9]

Steps:

  1. Download and install EaseUS PDF Editor
  2. Click Tools in the left panel, then select OCR
  3. Choose recognition mode, language, output format, and storage path
  4. Click Recognize to start[citation:9]

Option B: PDFpen (Mac)

PDFpen includes built-in OCR for Mac users. When you open a scanned PDF, it will automatically ask if you want to run OCR to convert the scan to editable text.[citation:9]

Option C: Online OCR Tools

Smallpdf and similar online platforms also offer OCR features for scanning PDFs. Simply upload the file, and the OCR engine will start automatically after detecting scanned text.[citation:9]

Privacy warning: Using online tools for scanned sensitive documents still carries the same privacy concerns as regular online conversion. If your scans contain personal information, financial data, or business secrets, choose a local desktop solution with built-in OCR.[citation:4][citation:12]

Best practice for scanned PDFs:

  • Scan at 300 DPI minimum for best OCR results
  • Keep pages straight (skewed pages reduce accuracy)
  • Use local software like WPS Office or EaseUS that processes OCR on your device, not in the cloud[citation:4]

Troubleshooting: Common Conversion Problems

Problem: Formatting looks wrong

Solution: Try a tool with a stronger layout preservation engine, such as WPS Office or Adobe Acrobat. For small formatting issues, manual adjustment may be faster than re-converting with another tool.[citation:4]

Problem: Conversion fails or is stuck

Possible causes:

  • File is password-protected (remove the password first)[citation:2]
  • File is corrupted[citation:2]
  • File is too large (try splitting it first)[citation:2]
  • Network issues (for online tools)[citation:2]

Problem: Fonts look wrong

Solution: Select all text in Word and apply a system font like Arial or Times New Roman. For new PDFs, ensure fonts are embedded when creating the PDF.[citation:6]

Privacy Note

Never upload sensitive documents to unknown online tools. When converting contracts, financial records, tax returns, medical information, or business plans, always choose a local solution such as WPS Office, Adobe Acrobat, or Foxit PDF Editor. These process your files on your device, not in the cloud.[citation:4][citation:12]

Even platforms that claim to delete files automatically cannot guarantee that your data wasn't intercepted or stored during processing.
